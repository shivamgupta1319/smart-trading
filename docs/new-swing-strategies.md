# Swing Trading Strategies

## Momentum & Breakout Strategies

These setups are designed to catch explosive, multi-week moves. They work exceptionally well in mid-caps, small-caps, and sectors experiencing heavy institutional inflows.

- **1. Volatility Contraction Pattern (VCP):** Popularized by Mark Minervini, this involves finding a stock that is consolidating in a progressively tightening range (e.g., a 15% pullback, then an 8% pullback, then a 3% pullback) while volume aggressively dries up. This signals that sellers are exhausted. You enter the moment the price breaks out of the final tight range on heavy volume.
- **2. Episodic Pivots (Earnings/Catalyst Gaps):** When a company announces shock earnings or major regulatory news (common in PSUs and defense), the stock will gap up massively. Instead of fading the gap or chasing the first day, wait for the stock to consolidate for 3 to 5 days. Enter when it breaks out of that short consolidation, riding the institutional accumulation phase.
- **3. The Break and Retest:** A stock breaks a multi-month or 52-week resistance level. Instead of buying the breakout, wait for the inevitable pullback where the price returns to test the _former_ resistance level, which should now act as support. Enter on the bounce.
- **4. The 10/50 EMA Crossover:** A momentum confirmation strategy. You go long when the fast 10-day Exponential Moving Average (EMA) crosses above the 50-day EMA. Because crossovers are lagging, this works best when combined with a breakout from a long consolidation base.

## Trend-Following & Pullback Strategies

The most consistent way to swing trade is to find an existing, powerful trend and wait for a low-risk entry point.

- **5. The 20-DMA Pullback:** The bread and butter of swing trading. In a strong uptrend, institutional algorithms step in to buy at the 20-Day Moving Average. Wait for a trending stock to pull back to its 20-DMA on _lower than average volume_ and enter when it prints a bullish reversal candlestick (like a hammer or engulfing pattern) off the line.
- **6. Fibonacci "Golden Zone" Retracements:** After a massive, impulsive leg up, plot a Fibonacci retracement from the swing low to the swing high. Place your limit buy orders in the "Golden Zone"—between the 50% and 61.8% retracement levels. This is where primary trends typically resume.
- **7. Multi-Timeframe Alignment:** Never take a swing trade based purely on a daily chart. If you spot a daily buy setup, zoom out to the weekly chart. If the weekly chart is in a downtrend (trading below its 50-week moving average), skip the trade. Only take trades where the daily setup aligns with the weekly macro trend.

## Mean Reversion & Structural Strategies

These are contrarian setups used when markets are overextended or trapped in wide trading ranges.

- **8. RSI Divergence at Major Support:** Price makes a lower low, but the RSI (14) makes a _higher_ low while in oversold territory (under 30). This divergence indicates that the downward momentum is dying. If this happens at a major weekly support level, it offers an incredible risk-to-reward ratio for a swing long.
- **9. Channel Oscillation Trading:** Heavyweight stocks (like Reliance or HDFC Bank) often get stuck in wide, multi-month channels. Buy exactly at the established bottom trendline and sell exactly at the top trendline. The edge here comes from the ability to place a very tight stop-loss just 1% below the channel support.
- **10. Volume Climax Capitulation:** Look for a prolonged downtrend that ends with a massive, panic-driven red candle accompanied by the highest volume of the year. This "capitulation volume" often means weak hands have panicked and strong hands have absorbed all the supply. Enter when the price manages to close above the high of the capitulation candle.
